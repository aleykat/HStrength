// Before You Begin

A safe start comes first

Two minutes before you pick up a weight. This isn't red tape — it's how you make sure this plan is right for you today, and what to watch for so you can train with confidence. This is a fitness plan, not medical advice.

Quick self-screen

Tick any that are true for you. There's no wrong answer — this just tells you whether a quick chat with your doctor should come first.

In the last while…
Worth a quick check first. You ticked at least one box — please talk with your physician before starting, and bring this plan with you. It doesn't mean you can't do this; it means a 10-minute conversation makes your start safer. The ankle box in particular is why ankle/ortho PT input is recommended.
Nothing ticked? You're clear to begin gently. Still, stop and get advice if any of the red-flag signs below show up once you're moving.

Stop immediately if you feel…

Stop the session and seek medical advice

• Chest pain or pressure
• Dizziness, faintness, or lightheadedness
• An irregular, racing, or fluttering heartbeat
• Sharp or sudden joint pain — different from normal muscle effort
• Unusual shortness of breath, or sudden heat / sharp pain / swelling in your ankle

Call emergency services for chest pain or pressure, severe shortness of breath, or fainting. For the rest: stop, rest, and check in with a professional before training again.

When to bring in a professional

Three people are worth knowing about. You don't need all of them — these are the specific signals that make a visit worthwhile.

Physician / GP

Before starting if you ticked any self-screen box. Any red-flag symptom above. New or worsening pain that doesn't settle within a few days, or any heart/breathing concern.

Pelvic-floor PT

Any leaking, pressure, heaviness, or bulging during or after effort — especially if it happens more than once. Reinforce the exhale-on-effort habit meanwhile. Common and very treatable.

Ankle / ortho PT

Ankle swelling that keeps recurring (repeated Yellow/Red), pain that isn't improving, or instability and low confidence that isn't progressing through the ankle ladder.

The honest disclaimer

This program is general fitness guidance, not medical advice, and doesn't replace care from your physician or a physiotherapist. You know your body and your history best — when something here conflicts with professional advice you've been given, follow the professional advice.

// Start Here

How to use this plan

Read this once, then you mostly live in two places: the Sessions (what to do today) and the Tracker (where you log it). Everything else is here when you need it.

Two sessions

Session A and Session B. Alternate them — A one session, B the next. Either one stands alone if a week gets cut short.

Warm up, every time

The warm-up and the ankle drill are never-cut items. If time is short, drop a set from the main work — never the warm-up.

Breathe out on effort

Exhale on the hard part of every rep. Never hold your breath under load. This protects your pelvic floor.

The first four weeks

Some soreness is normal — the dull, all-over kind that eases within a day or two. Leaving two or three reps "in the tank" is correct, not lazy. The first session should feel almost too easy. On a rough day there's a named 10-Minute Session that still counts. The goal of Week 1 is to finish feeling good — not wrecked.

What a good session looks like

A good session is not a heavy one, an exhausting one, or one that leaves you sore for days. It's much simpler than that:

You showed up

You warmed up, did your ankle drill, and moved — even if it was the 10-minute version on a tired day. Showing up is the win that builds the habit.

You moved well

Controlled reps, exhaling on effort, stopping a couple of reps short of a struggle. Quality over grind, every time.

You finished worked, not wrecked

A little warm and a little tired — not destroyed. If you're wiped out or sore for days, the next one was too much. Ease off; that's information, not failure.

You logged it

Thirty seconds in the tracker. That record is what turns scattered workouts into visible, undeniable progress.

Milestones worth celebrating

The scale is the slowest, noisiest signal there is — and not the point here. These markers tell you the plan is working, often long before the scale does:

First weight added

The first time the double-progression rule earns you the next PowerBlock increment. Proof you got stronger.

First pain-free session

A whole session — ankle included — with no nagging discomfort. A milestone you'll feel, not measure.

Four straight weeks

Both sessions, four weeks running. This is the one that predicts everything that follows.

Daily life feels easier

Stairs, carrying groceries, getting off the floor — the moment one of these is noticeably easier than it used to be.

Standing on one leg

First unassisted single-leg balance (Ankle Ladder Stage 3) — real confidence rebuilt in that joint.

First steady 20-minute ride

If you took up the optional cardio — a comfortable 20 minutes in the saddle.

// The Sessions

What you do each day

Each session is about 30 minutes: warm-up, a short activation, 15 minutes of main work, your ankle drill, and a brief cooldown. Tap a session to see the full breakdown.

Choosing your starting weight — start very light, on purpose

Each exercise below carries a suggested starting weight, and they're deliberately low. The first session should feel almost too easy — finish each set thinking "I could have done several more." That is exactly right: the goal for the first few weeks is building the habit, not chasing load. The double-progression rule will add weight when you've earned it. If in doubt, go lighter — it's far easier (and safer for your ankle and pelvic floor) to add a little next time than to start too heavy and get sore enough to skip the next session. These numbers are a starting point; after a session or two, let how it feels (RPE 5–6) be your guide.

The 10-Minute Session — a real session, not a consolation prize

For rough days, flare days, and days when something is better than nothing. 5 min abbreviated warm-up (marching + arm circles, then seated ankle alphabet & heel/toe raises — don't skip the ankle part) → pick 2 exercises from today's session, 1 set each at a 4–5/10 effort → done. On a Session A day: Goblet Squat 1×10–15 + Dead Bug 1×6–8/side. On a Session B day: RDL 1×10–15 + Bird-Dog 1×6–8/side. Mark "Min dose" on your tracker. You showed up. You have not broken anything.

// Exercise Library

The movements

Each card tells you how to do it, how to breathe, what it asks of your ankle, and how to make it easier or harder. The colored dot shows ankle (dorsiflexion) demand: ● low   ● moderate   ● high. Seated and floor exercises carry no ankle load.

A note on overhead pressing

Overhead pressing isn't in the program yet — your shoulders need time to open up before loading them vertically. When you can do a pain-free wall slide AND you've completed 6 weeks of incline pressing with no shoulder complaint, you're ready. There's no rush: the incline press gives you most of the benefit with none of the risk right now.

// Warm-Up & Mobility

Seven minutes before any weight

Run through these in order, every session. It wakes up your back, shoulders, hips, and ankle before you load anything. Go gently — you're thawing out, not stretching hard.

Never cut this

If your session is running short, cut a set from the main-work supersets — never the warm-up. Arriving cold at a loaded exercise is how tweaks happen.

Blood moving · 2–3 min

  • March in place, arms swinging
  • Arm circles: 10 forward, 10 back
  • Shoulder rolls: 10 each way
  • Gentle neck rolls side to side

Hips & low back · 2 min

  • Single knee-to-chest — 5s hold, 3× each side
  • Double knee-to-chest — gentle rock side to side
  • Cat-cow — 8 slow reps with your breath
  • Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch — 10–15s each side

Shoulders · 1 min

  • Arm circles — 10 small + 10 large, each way
  • Wall slides — 8–10 slow reps (also your overhead-press readiness check)
  • Towel / band pull-aparts — 10 reps

Ankle prep · 2 min

  • Seated ankle alphabet — trace A–Z, both feet
  • Seated heel/toe raises — 2 × 10 each
  • Gentle calf stretch — 15s each side, slight bend in back knee
Movement preview · 1 min

5 wall-touch hip hinges (push hips back to touch a wall) + 5 air squats (heels on your platform). These tell you how your body feels today — if something feels off, adjust your starting weight accordingly.

// The Plan

How you get stronger

Strength comes from loading consistently, giving a little more than last time, and recovering well. The way you do that here needs just one decision.

The double-progression rule
When you complete all sets at the top of the rep range for 2 consecutive sessions with good form, add the next PowerBlock increment and return to the bottom of the range.

That's the whole rule. No percentages, no max-effort testing. Example (Block 1, range 12–15): hit 2×15 with clean form and a rep or two to spare → do it again next Session A → then add 2.5 lb and drop back to 12, and climb back up to 15 at the new weight.

When NOT to add weight

Reps fell short of the top last time · form broke down · it felt like a genuine 8/10. Staying put and adding a rep is progress.

Increments

Always try the smallest jump first — 2.5 lb. At lighter weights that's a real percentage bump. Give any new weight 2–3 sessions before calling it "too light."

The rule is a ceiling

It's the maximum speed of progression — never a requirement. A day you stayed at the same weight and moved well is a good session.

Bad-day override

If your ankle is Yellow/Red, energy is very low, or something aches: don't increase weight. Stay where you are or use the 10-Minute Session. For lower-body increases, wait until you've had two consecutive Green ankle sessions.

Signs you need an unplanned deload

Persistent joint ache (not the muscle soreness that clears in a day or two) · lifts stalling 2+ weeks at the same weight and reps · motivation dropping sharply for no clear reason. Take a recovery week and come back at reduced load. That's the plan working as designed.

When to adjust on the fly

Yellow / Red ankle

Seated/supported exercises only that session, drop to 2 sets, follow the swelling care. Don't advance a ladder stage until two Green sessions.

High fatigue / soreness

Cut to 2 sets, stay at the bottom of the rep range, use an easy weight. A lighter day, not a wasted one.

Joint pain in a movement

Stop that exercise, note it, swap in the named regression. Two sessions in a row → address it, don't push through.

Missed a session

Don't add volume to make up for it. Resume the planned session as written. One miss changes nothing.

Everything feels easy

Green light to progress — but only if you've hit the top of the range for 2 consecutive sessions. Follow the rule.

Pelvic floor signal

Leaking, pressure, or heaviness → scale the load now, reinforce exhale-on-effort every rep. More than once → see a pelvic-floor physiotherapist. Common and treatable.

// Optional Cardio

Easy miles, when you want them

Cycling here is completely optional and comfort-first — one easy spin on an off-day, never a substitute for a strength session or your ankle drills. The saddle is the only real barrier, so we solve that first. This isn't "a lot of cycling" — it's gentle, joint-friendly movement you might enjoy.

How it fits

If you'd like to ride, aim for one easy spin on a day you're not lifting — for example, strength on Monday and Thursday, an optional spin on Saturday. Skip it freely on a busy week or a sore-ankle day. It's a bonus, not a box to tick.

Before your first ride — set up for comfort

Five minutes of setup makes the difference between "that was pleasant" and "I'm never sitting on that again." Do this before you clip in — or in your case, before you hop on.

Saddle height

At the bottom of the pedal stroke your knee should keep a slight bend (roughly 25–30°) — not locked straight, not cramped up. Your hips should stay level, not rock side to side. Too high rocks the hips; too low crowds the knees.

Fore / aft

With the pedals level (3 & 9 o'clock), the front of your forward knee should sit roughly over the pedal axle. Slide the saddle forward or back to get there.

Saddle tilt

Keep the saddle level, or tip the nose down just a hair (1–2°) if you feel pressure up front. Never nose-up — that's the main comfort killer.

Padded shorts

A pair of chamois (padded cycling) shorts, worn with nothing underneath, is the single biggest comfort upgrade. Sit-bone soreness in the first couple of weeks is normal adaptation — not damage.

Foot position — protect your ankle (flat pedals)

Place the ball of your foot over the pedal axle — not your arch or heel. Keep your ankle neutral at the bottom of the stroke: don't drive the heel down hard or point your toes. A relaxed, level foot keeps the repaired ankle happy. Pedal smooth circles, not stomps.

Let the trainer do the work — ERG mode

In Zwift, ride in ERG mode with the target set to your wattage below. The Kickr Core then holds that power automatically — you don't shift gears or fight resistance, you just pedal at a comfortable cadence (around 70–80 rpm). It's the most relaxed, comfort-first way to ride indoors.

The watt number serves you, not the other way around. Every target below should feel easy enough to hold a full conversation (an easy Zone 1–2 effort). If you can't talk in sentences, the wattage is too high — drop it. These numbers are deliberately low starting points, not goals to chase.

Duration & power ramp

A gentle, optional progression — think roughly one ride a week, but there's no schedule to keep. Repeat any step as long as you like before moving up. If a ride ever feels like work, stay where you are.

Comfort, ankle & when to skip

Saddle comfort

Early on, lift off the saddle for a few seconds every few minutes. Soreness that fades within a day is normal adaptation. Numbness or persistent pain means stop and tweak the fit (usually tilt or height) or shorten the ride.

Your ankle comes first

If your ankle is Yellow or Red (see Swelling), skip the spin — cycling is optional and your ankle ladder takes priority. Keep the foot neutral, the cadence easy, and the gears light; never grind.

Pelvic floor

Same habit as your lifting: relaxed breathing, no straining. If you feel pressure or heaviness, ease the watts back. Gentle spinning shouldn't provoke it.

It's a bonus, never a debt

A missed ride costs you nothing — there's nothing to make up. Cardio never replaces a strength session or your ankle drills. On a good week it's a pleasant add-on; on a hard week it's the first thing to drop.
